Overview

I am a professor and Deputy Chairperson in the Department of Business & Information Systems at Bronx Community College (CUNY), where I also coordinate the Accounting curriculum and support program assessment efforts. I am also a proud Bronx Community College alumnus from the Class of 1989, and I have spent more than three decades serving the institution that helped shape my academic and professional journey.

My work is grounded in a simple goal: helping students move forward with confidence. Whether that is mastering a challenging accounting concept, building stronger study habits, or mapping out a realistic academic and career plan, I try to bring clarity, structure, and encouragement to the process.

Teaching

My teaching focuses primarily on financial and intermediate accounting, with an emphasis on clear reasoning, careful problem-solving, and real-world application. I aim to create a learning environment that is supportive but rigorous—one where students are challenged, but also feel that the path to improvement is visible and achievable.

  • Instructional focus: fundamentals that scale into more advanced accounting work
  • Classroom approach: step-by-step structure, practice-driven mastery, and active feedback
  • Student support: consistent check-ins, review sessions, and guidance on academic habits

If you are a student: my goal is for you to leave the course more confident with intermediate-level accounting issues and better able to read financial statements in greater depth.

Teaching Philosophy

A quick snapshot of how I teach, and what students can expect.

My teaching philosophy centers on the belief that students deserve clear, engaging instruction within a supportive learning environment. I emphasize active participation, collaboration, and consistent interaction with both peers and the instructor. I use a teaching framework I developed called Learn, Understand, then Do (LUD), which guides students from foundational knowledge, to conceptual understanding, and finally to practical application.

Full Statement

My teaching philosophy is grounded in the belief that students deserve exceptional instruction within an engaging and dynamic learning environment. I strive to create a classroom experience where students actively participate, collaborate with one another, and feel comfortable engaging with the instructor.

I firmly believe that with willingness, focus, and determination to achieve their goals—combined with a teacher who prioritizes student needs—students can achieve meaningful academic success. My role is to provide structure, clarity, and guidance while encouraging students to take ownership of their learning.

To support this process, I use a teaching framework I developed called Learn, Understand, then Do (LUD). This approach emphasizes a structured progression in learning:

  • Learn — Students begin by acquiring foundational knowledge through lectures, readings, and guided instruction.
  • Understand — Students then deepen their comprehension by analyzing concepts, asking questions, and connecting ideas.
  • Do — Finally, students apply what they have learned through problem-solving, practical examples, and real-world scenarios.

The LUD approach reinforces learning by combining theory with application, helping students move beyond memorization toward true understanding and skill development.

My sincere goal is for every student to succeed. To accomplish this, I encourage students to be proactive and immersed in the learning process. If confusion or challenges arise, I urge students to reach out early—whether through in-person conversations or email communication. I also encourage collaboration among students so they can support one another throughout the semester.

While I am fully invested in each student’s success, meaningful progress requires initiative from the student as well. Addressing questions and concerns early leads to stronger understanding, greater confidence, and a more positive learning and assessment experience.

Prof. Paul Jaijairam, Deputy Chairperson

Student FAQ: What to Expect in My Classes

Clear expectations + real support. I’m here to help you succeed — and I also expect you to be accountable.

Read the Student FAQ (“What to expect in my classes”) ⌄
Quick mindset: You don’t have to be “perfect” to do well — but you do have to be consistent. Read before class, show up, ask questions early, and use support (office hours/tutoring) when needed.
1) What should I do before coming to class?

Read the assigned chapter before class. Class time is designed to reinforce, clarify, and apply what you read— not introduce it for the first time. Come in ready to participate.

2) Is attendance really important?

Yes. Each session builds on the last. When you miss class, you miss explanations, examples, and patterns that help concepts “click.” Attendance is one of the strongest predictors of success.

3) What do you expect from me during class?

Active participation: follow steps, attempt problems, ask questions, and stay engaged. You’re not here to watch accounting—you’re here to do it.

4) What if I’m confused?

Reach out early. Don’t wait until after an exam. Confusion is normal—silence is what creates problems. Ask in class, email me, come to office hours, or use tutoring.

5) How often can I email you?

As often as needed—especially if you are actively working and have specific questions. Clear communication is part of being successful.

6) Are office hours only for students who are struggling?

Not at all. Office hours are for anyone who wants clarity, stronger habits, better exam preparation, or guidance about programs and careers.

7) When should I use tutoring?

Use tutoring if you feel consistently unsure, if your quiz/exam results show gaps, or if I recommend it. Tutoring is proactive—not a last resort.

8) What if I miss a class?

It’s your responsibility to catch up. Exchange contact information with classmates early. Get notes, review posted materials, then email me if questions remain.

9) How should I prepare for exams?

Rework practice problems without looking at solutions. If you can explain each step and why it’s done, you’re ready. If you’re only memorizing, you’re not.

10) What do you mean by student accountability?

I will support you fully—but you must do your part: prepare, attend, participate, and communicate early when you need help. This is how real progress happens.

Course-level expectations (choose the one that applies)
First-Year / Principles courses (ACC 111 / ACC 112): what matters most ⌄

In first-year accounting, your biggest job is building strong habits and a strong foundation. Small gaps can grow quickly if ignored. Success comes from consistency.

  • Read before class so you recognize terms and steps (accounting is a new language at first).
  • Practice frequently (short practice sessions beat cramming every time).
  • Focus on the “why”, not just the final answer.
  • Use tutoring early if you feel lost—don’t wait until you “fall behind.”
  • Ask questions out loud (many students have the same question).
Simple rule: if you’re not sure about a topic, address it the same week it’s taught.
Intermediate / Advanced courses: what changes (and what doesn’t) ⌄

Intermediate/advanced accounting moves faster and requires more judgment. You’ll see multi-step problems and deeper analysis, and the “details” matter. Reading and preparation become even more important.

  • Pre-class reading is non-negotiable — class is where we apply and analyze.
  • Bring questions (even short ones). Questions are evidence of preparation.
  • Expect multi-step work — you must show reasoning, not just answers.
  • Practice exam-style problems under time constraints.
  • Use office hours strategically for deeper topics and study planning.
Simple rule: if you can explain your steps clearly, you are building the skill level the course requires.
How this ties to LUD: Learn by reading before class, Understand through class discussion and questions, and Do through practice problems, tutoring, office hours, and exam preparation.

Program Assessment & Continuous Improvement

I care deeply about assessment as a practical tool—not as paperwork. At its best, assessment helps us see what is working, where students are struggling, and what changes will improve the learning experience. My approach is to keep it grounded, evidence-based, and usable for faculty.

  • Focus: clear outcomes, meaningful measures, and actionable results
  • Goal: use findings to strengthen instruction and support students more effectively
  • Mindset: keep processes simple, repeatable, and transparent
